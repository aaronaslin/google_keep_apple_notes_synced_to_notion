There is no existence without circles. It's seeing the circles clearly that frees us up from mental suffering. Our ultimate power is in the circles we choose, accept, wear, inhabit, take on, embody, live in freely, without attachment. As long as their is a choice being made, their is attachment and ego behind it. Being isn't a choice. Fish don't chose to swim nor birds choose to fly. Being present is to freely be the circle of the moment while just as freely let the circle die/go/erase, in the next moment.

Emptiness isn't the end state, emptiness is the path, tool, state that opens or unlocks all the doors around you. In emptiness you are free to notice without interpretation. How you react is the circle you inhabit.

There is no world where you are removed from action. And in every interaction the world changes. Something dies and something blooms. When Mingyur was describing the station experience he doesnt mention the eyes of anyone around him, or the reaction he gives them. Instead of erasing the circles of status, safety, cleanliness, he soldifies them, separating himself from the open experience. In the train he could have seen through the reality for what it is... Humans with the capacity for unbridled love, worthy of love. If he had looked into the eyes of each individual and connected with them, how might his train experience changed? How might his attachment to safety, status and cleanliness shifted? Would he have felt alone or would he have felt the deep connection between us all? In his choice to turn inward, seeking a state of abiding, he missed an opportunity for radiating that abiding to those around him, making himself and the world more full of love.

I am not a pet owner. When my pet dies my identity of a pet owner doesn't die too. The pet activities cease to exist. But when my pet needs food, I feed it. When it needs exercise, I walk it. However, I am a parent. When my child dies so too does my label as parent.

This is ego and this separates the husband from the actual reality via cognitive distortions and biases that ensure his role is protected. But the good husband understands the role IS to be present to the needs of his wife and act on those needs when the moment invites it. And to do this you must be present in abiding.


I have been incredibly good at inventing and creating stories in my head about the people around me, the world around me, the way things should be instead of the way they are. When this view is challenged I've felt slighted, I've deflected, I've discounted, twisted, ignored, or even attacked the person bringing the idea. And I've believed that this world view around me is real. I'm articulate because I'm a really good story teller.... And yet I've always wondered how people create such elaborate stories and fantasies believing that fiction is fiction and non-fiction is fact. But I've realized the stories Ive been telling myself are as fictional as a fantasy story when I thought they were the truth. Whoa.

Since realizing this I've realized how much I've used my charisma to spin a story a room an experience into what reality is. I've been bad at actually hearing and listening and understanding what the perceived reality is, the needs are, and the wants are of those around me. I've thought arrogantly how if they only got what was in my head everything would be better. And better yet, they'd realize I was right.

Since realizing this, ive had a desire for quietness. Open to see the world for what it is. I feel like I need to learn a new language. I need to learn how to ask new questions. Learn how to be present. Learn how to sit with other people and where they are at. I realize too how hard phone conversations can be ... How much they have pushed towards explaining the reality in my head and not reality. It's hard to explain reality when you don't have needs. It's not necessary to talk about reality. All there is is being together in the present moment.

Talking about the past dwells on the last. Talking about the future dwells on the future. And in dwelling on the past and on the future we are opening up the stories we tell ourselves about both. The more we dwell the less we are in reality and in the present moment.
